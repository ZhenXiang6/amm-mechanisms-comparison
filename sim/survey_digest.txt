TOPIC: Uniswap V2 Constant-Product Automated Market Maker (CPMM/CFMM): invariant, fee-adjusted swaps, pricing, slippage, impermanent loss, and LP-token mechanics

SUMMARY:
Uniswap V2 is the canonical constant-product market maker (CPMM), a member of the broader constant-function market maker (CFMM) family. Each pair contract holds reserves x and y of two ERC-20 tokens and enforces the invariant x·y = k: a trade is admissible only if it leaves the product of reserves at or above its prior value. Equivalently, the reachable reserve states lie on the hyperbola of constant k, so the AMM is defined by the trading function φ(x,y)=x·y rather than by an order book. Angeris et al. (2019/2020) formalize this as a market with reserves R_α, R_β, constant product k=R_α·R_β, and percentage fee (1−γ); a trade tendering Δ_β to receive Δ_α must satisfy (R_α−Δ_α)(R_β+γΔ_β)=k (their eq. 1). The whitepaper (Adams, Zinsmeister, Salem, Keefer, Robinson 2020) implements the same idea with γ=0.997, charging a 30-basis-point (0.3%) fee on the input.

The fee is applied to the input amount, not the output. With reserves (x,y), an input dx of token X returns dy = 997·y·dx / (1000·x + 997·dx). This is algebraically identical to enforcing (x + 0.997·dx)(y − dy) = x·y, i.e. only 99.7% of the input is credited to the curve while the full input is added to reserves, so k strictly grows and the 0.3% accrues to LPs (verified: with x=1000, y=2000, dx=50 the post-trade reserve product equals the pre-trade product to machine precision). The whitepaper's on-chain check is the fee-adjusted invariant (x1 − 0.003·x_in)·y1 ≥ x0·y0 (eq. 9), generalized for flash swaps and scaled by 1,000,000 to integer form (1000·x1 − 3·x_in)(1000·y1 − 3·y_in) ≥ 10^6·x0·y0 (eqs. 10–11). A footnote notes the fee measured against the withdrawn amount is slightly higher: 1/(1−0.003) − 1 = 3/997 ≈ 0.30092%.

The marginal (spot) price of X in terms of Y, ignoring fees, is p = y/x = R_β/R_α — the limit price of an infinitesimal trade obtained by differentiating the invariant. Angeris et al. show the fee shifts the effective marginal price to γ^{-1}·m_u where m_u = R_β/R_α, and that under no-arbitrage the Uniswap price tracks the reference price m_p within a factor of γ (the bound m_u ≥ γ·m_p), giving a γ:1/γ no-arbitrage band — the AMM's bid/ask spread. The realized (average execution) price of a finite trade, dy/dx = 997·y/(1000·x + 997·dx), is always worse than the spot price because the price moves monotonically along the curve as the trade executes; the gap is price impact/slippage. To first order, ignoring fees, fractional price impact ≈ dx/x for small trades, and the exact (feeless) execution-price degradation is (spot−realized)/spot = (dx/x)/(1 + dx/x), so slippage grows roughly linearly in trade size relative to the reserve and is unbounded as dx grows (the pool can never be fully drained).

For a 50/50 pool, impermanent loss (divergence loss) compares the value of LP-deposited assets against simply holding them when the external price ratio changes by r = P'/P. Because reserves rebalance along x·y=k, the pool always sells the appreciating asset and buys the depreciating one, yielding IL(r) = 2·√r/(1+r) − 1 ≤ 0, with equality only at r=1. The derivation: holding x0 tokens worth P each plus y0=P·x0 numeraire has value V_hold = y0 + x0·P'; the pool at the new price holds x'=√(k/P'), y'=√(k·P') with value V_pool = 2√(k·P'), and V_pool/V_hold reduces to 2√r/(1+r). Numerically IL is symmetric in r↔1/r (−5.72% at r=2 or r=0.5, −20% at r=4 or r=1/4), confirmed by the direct value computation. LP positions are tokenized: the first deposit mints √(x·y) liquidity tokens (minus a permanently-locked MINIMUM_LIQUIDITY of 1000 units), and subsequent deposits mint s = (dx/x)·totalSupply = (dy/y)·totalSupply, requiring proportional deposits; burning returns the same pro-rata share of both reserves, including accumulated fees. The optional protocol fee (feeOn) captures 1/6 of LP fees by minting LP tokens proportional to the growth of √k since the last collection (whitepaper eqs. 4–7).

KEY FACTS:

 - The fee is 0.3% (30 basis points) charged on the INPUT amount; the retention factor is gamma = 997/1000 = 0.997, giving swap output dy = 997*y*dx/(1000*x + 997*dx).
 - The whitepaper expresses this as the fee-adjusted invariant (x1 - 0.003*x_in)*y1 >= x0*y0, scaled to integer form (1000*x1 - 3*x_in)(1000*y1 - 3*y_in) >= 1e6*x0*y0 to avoid floating point on-chain.
 - Measured against the withdrawn amount the effective fee is slightly higher: 1/(1-0.003) - 1 = 3/997 ≈ 0.30092% (whitepaper footnote).
 - Spot/marginal price is p = y/x = R_beta/R_alpha (limit of an infinitesimal trade); the fee shifts the effective marginal price to gamma^{-1}*(y/x).
 - Realized average execution price dy/dx = 997*y/(1000*x + 997*dx) is always worse than spot; the difference is price impact/slippage, which grows ~linearly in dx/x for small trades and is unbounded as the trade size grows (verified: x=1000,y=2000,dx=50 gives 5.03% feeless slippage).

 - Uniswap V2 is a constant-FUNCTION market maker (CFMM) with trading function phi(x,y)=x*y; the pricing function maps reserves to marginal price, generalizing to other CFMMs (e.g. Balancer weighted, Curve stableswap).

TOPIC: Uniswap V3 Concentrated Liquidity: Mathematics of Virtual/Real Reserves, Tick Math, Capital Efficiency, Impermanent Loss, and Fee Accounting

SUMMARY:
Uniswap V3 (Adams, Zinsmeister, Salem, Keefer, Robinson, 2021) generalizes the constant-product market maker by letting each liquidity provider concentrate liquidity within an arbitrary price interval [p_a, p_b] rather than across (0, infinity). Within its active range, a position behaves exactly like a V2 constant-product pool operating on VIRTUAL reserves (x*y = k, equation 2.1), but it only needs to hold the REAL reserves required to support trading inside the range. The whitepaper parameterizes liquidity by L = sqrt(k) = sqrt(x*y) (so L^2 = x*y in virtual reserves), and tracks the square-root price sqrt(P) instead of P. The real-reserve curve is a translation of the constant-product hyperbola, (x_real + L/sqrt(p_b))(y_real + L*sqrt(p_a)) = L^2 (equation 2.2), which is exactly solvent at both range endpoints: at p_b the position is entirely token1 (y), at p_a it is entirely token0 (x). The key linear relationship between liquidity and price movement is L = dY/d(sqrt(P)) (equation 6.7), giving the swap-amount identities d(sqrt(P)) = dy/L (6.13), dy = d(sqrt(P))*L (6.14), d(1/sqrt(P)) = dx/L (6.15), and dx = d(1/sqrt(P))*L (6.16). From these, the per-position real token holdings at current price P inside the range are x = L*(1/sqrt(P) - 1/sqrt(p_b)) and y = L*(sqrt(P) - sqrt(p_a)) (Atis Elsts technical note Eqs. 11-12, equivalent to whitepaper Eqs. 6.29-6.30 with dL = L).

The continuous price space is discretized by TICKS: tick i corresponds to price p(i) = 1.0001^i (equation 6.1), so each tick is a 1-basis-point (0.01%) multiplicative step. Because the pool tracks square-root prices, sqrt(p(i)) = 1.0001^(i/2) (equation 6.2), and the current tick is i_c = floor(log_{1.0001} sqrt(P)) (equation 6.8). Not every tick may be a position boundary: a pool's immutable tickSpacing parameter t_s restricts initializable ticks to multiples of t_s. Tick spacing is paired with the fee tier: 0.01%/tickSpacing 1, 0.05%/tickSpacing 10 (~0.10% between initializable ticks), 0.30%/tickSpacing 60 (~0.60%), and 1%/tickSpacing 200 (~2.02%). Wider spacing reduces gas (fewer initialized ticks crossed) at the cost of coarser range granularity.

The CAPITAL-EFFICIENCY multiplier of a concentrated position relative to providing the same capital across the full V2 curve is 1/(1 - (p_a/p_b)^{1/4}), as stated directly by Uniswap founder Hayden Adams: a position bounded in [p_a, p_b] can offer the same depth (same L) at the current price using a fraction 1 - (p_a/p_b)^{1/4} of the capital a V2 position would require. As p_a/p_b -> 1 (an infinitesimally tight range), the multiplier diverges; the whitepaper cites up to ~4000x for very tight ranges. For example, an ETH/BTC range from 0.0295 to 0.0454 yields roughly 9.8x efficiency.

IMPERMANENT LOSS in V3 is qualitatively the same divergence loss as V2 but is AMPLIFIED while the price stays inside the range, because the position supplies the same trading depth with less capital (the multiplier above scales the exposure). Once price exits the range, the position stops earning fees and is fully converted into a single asset: 100% token0 (x) if P <= p_a, or 100% token1 (y) if P >= p_b. Beyond the range the position no longer tracks price (it is a static single-asset holding), so its mark-to-market value diverges further from a HODL portfolio than an equivalent in-range exposure would. Concentrating into tighter ranges therefore trades higher fee income (greater capital efficiency, more fees per dollar while in range) against higher IL and the risk of going inactive. Static replication of this payoff via European options is given by Deng, Zong & Wang (2022).

FEE ACCOUNTING is per-position and non-compounding (fees accrue as separate token balances, not as added liquidity). The pool tracks global accumulators feeGrowthGlobal0/1 (f_{g,0}, f_{g,1}), the all-time fees per unit of virtual liquidity L. Each swap increments them by df_{g,1} = y_in * gamma * (1 - phi) (equation 6.9), where gamma is the fee fraction and phi the protocol-fee fraction. Each tick stores feeGrowthOutside (f_o); fees-below f_b(i) and fees-above f_a(i) a tick depend on whether i_c >= i (equations 6.17-6.18), and the cumulative fee growth inside a range [i_l, i_u] is f_r = f_g - f_a(i_u) - f_b(i_l) (equation 6.19). A position stores feeGrowthInsideLast (f_r(t_0)); its uncollected fees in a token are f_u = l * (f_r(t_1) - f_r(t_0)) (equation 6.28), where l is the position's liquidity. This isolates each position's fee entitlement to exactly the periods its range was active.

KEY FACTS:
 - Liquidity is defined as L = sqrt(x*y) (L^2 = x*y) on the VIRTUAL reserves; the pool stores L (liquidity) and sqrt(P) (sqrtPriceX96), not x and y directly (whitepaper Eqs. 2.1, 6.3-6.6, Table 1).
 - The real-reserve curve (x_real + L/sqrt(p_b))(y_real + L*sqrt(p_a)) = L^2 (Eq. 2.2) is a translated constant-product hyperbola that is exactly solvent only within [p_a, p_b]; outside the range the position is 100% one asset.
 - L = dY/d(sqrt(P)) (Eq. 6.7): liquidity is the constant ratio of token1 change to square-root-price change within a tick, making swap math linear.
 - In-range token holdings are x = L*(1/sqrt(P) - 1/sqrt(p_b)) and y = L*(sqrt(P) - sqrt(p_a)); at P=p_b the position is all token1, at P=p_a all token0 (Elsts Eqs. 11-12).
 - Tick prices follow p(i) = 1.0001^i, so each tick is a 1-basis-point step; the pool tracks sqrt(p(i)) = 1.0001^(i/2) and current tick i_c = floor(log_{1.0001} sqrt(P)) (Eqs. 6.1, 6.2, 6.8).

 - A fourth tier, 0.01% with tickSpacing 1, was added by UNI governance (proposal passed November 2021), giving the full set 0.01% / 0.05% / 0.30% / 1% = 1 / 5 / 30 / 100 bps.
 - Capital-efficiency multiplier of a concentrated position vs. full-range V2 is 1/(1 - (p_a/p_b)^{1/4}) (stated by Hayden Adams); the whitepaper cites up to ~4000x for very tight ranges, and an ETH/BTC 0.0295-0.0454 range gives ~9.8x.
 - Impermanent loss is the same divergence-loss mechanism as V2 but amplified while in range (in proportion to the capital-efficiency factor) and, once price exits [p_a, p_b], the position is fully converted to a single asset and stops earning fees.

 - Per-position fees use feeGrowthInside f_r = f_g - f_a(i_u) - f_b(i_l) (Eqs. 6.17-6.19) and uncollected fees f_u = l*(f_r(t_1) - f_r(t_0)) (Eq. 6.28), crediting each position only for periods its range was active.

TOPIC: Curve StableSwap invariant and Curve V2 (CryptoSwap) dynamic-peg AMM: mathematics of amplification, slippage, impermanent loss, and repegging

SUMMARY:
Curve's StableSwap (Egorov, 2019) is an automated market maker engineered for assets that trade near a common peg (e.g. stablecoins or wrapped/staked variants of the same asset). Its design philosophy is to interpolate between two limiting invariants: the constant-sum invariant \sum x_i = const, which gives a price of exactly 1 and therefore zero slippage but cannot hold once the true price drifts from the peg, and the constant-product invariant \prod x_i = const (Uniswap), which always quotes a price and provides liquidity at all prices but with large slippage. Egorov constructs the intermediate curve by multiplying the constant-sum invariant by a "leverage" coefficient \chi D^{n-1} and adding it to the constant-product invariant. The result is constant-product when \chi = 0 and constant-sum when \chi = \infty. To support arbitrary prices, \chi is made dynamic: \chi = A \prod x_i / (D/n)^n, which equals the constant amplification A when the pool is perfectly balanced and decays toward 0 as the pool becomes imbalanced. Substituting this dynamic \chi yields the canonical StableSwap invariant A n^n \sum x_i + D = A D n^n + D^{n+1}/(n^n \prod x_i).

Here D is the invariant that parametrizes the curve; it equals the total amount of coins deposited when the pool is in equilibrium (all balances equal, D = n x_eq). For a given set of balances, D is found by an iterative converging (Newton's-method) solution because the invariant is a high-degree polynomial in D; once D is fixed, a trade swapping coin i into coin j is executed by solving the same invariant for the unknown output balance x_j (the "get-y given-x" step), again via Newton iteration using only integer arithmetic in the Vyper contract. The amplification A is the central tunable: low A approaches Uniswap (constant product), high A flattens the curve around the peg so that the marginal price -dx_i/dx_j stays very close to 1 over a wide range of balances. Egorov's original simulation on DAI/USDC/USDT over May-October 2019 found an optimal A = 85, fee 0.06%, giving roughly 312% APR in that backtest; the paper also cites a practical A = 100 ("Uniswap with 100x leverage"). Slippage is near-zero close to the peg because the dynamic \chi keeps the curve in its flat constant-sum-like region, but as the pool becomes imbalanced \chi collapses toward 0 and the invariant reverts to constant-product behavior, so the marginal price moves sharply and slippage rises steeply — this is the mechanism that protects the pool by widening prices when one asset is being depleted. Because the assets are mutually pegged and the curve is flat near 1, the divergence between pooled value and held value (impermanent loss) is very small for stable/pegged assets: IL only becomes material if the peg actually breaks, in which case the flat curve causes LPs to accumulate the depegging (cheaper) asset.

Curve V2 / CryptoSwap (Egorov, "Automatic market-making with dynamic peg", 2021) generalizes StableSwap to volatile, non-pegged assets (e.g. ETH/BTC/USD). It introduces a transformed price space: real balances b relate to transformed balances b' through a coefficient vector p called price_scale, b = (b'_0 p_0, b'_1 p_1, ...), so the pool concentrates StableSwap-like liquidity around the current internal price rather than around 1.0. The CurveCrypto invariant keeps the StableSwap algebraic form K D^{N-1} \sum x_i + \prod x_i = K D^N + (D/N)^N but redefines the coefficient K via K_0 = \prod x_i N^N / D^N and K = A K_0 \gamma^2 / (\gamma + 1 - K_0)^2, where A is amplification and \gamma > 0 (typically 10^{-4}) controls the distance/separation between the two constant-product branches and hence the width of the flat region. This makes the invariant behave approximately as a superposition of constant-product and StableSwap: near equilibrium (K_0 \approx 1) K is large and the curve is flat/StableSwap-like; far from equilibrium K collapses and the curve becomes constant-product, guaranteeing liquidity at all prices.

The "dynamic peg" is the repegging algorithm. An internal exponential-moving-average price oracle tracks the last traded price plast with EMA weight \alpha = 2^{-t/T_{1/2}}, p* = plast (1 - \alpha) + \alpha p*_prev. After each swap or liquidity event the contract nudges price_scale p in log-space toward p* by a small step s. Crucially, repegging is only allowed when it is profitable: the protocol measures portfolio value via the constant-product value at equilibrium X_cp = (D/N) / (\prod p_i)^{1/N} (a noise-free profit measure), tracks cumulative xcp_profit, and undoes any p-adjustment whose realized loss would exceed half of the profit accumulated so far. This "only repeg when loss < half the profit" rule lets the pool follow the market price over time while ensuring repegging never destroys more LP value than it has earned, yielding roughly 5-10x higher liquidity concentration than Uniswap x*y=k for volatile pairs. CryptoSwap also uses dynamic fees that scale from f_mid to f_out with pool imbalance via g = \gamma_{fee} / (\gamma_{fee} + 1 - \prod x_i / ((\sum x_i)/N)^N), f = g f_mid + (1 - g) f_out. Because CryptoSwap holds volatile, uncorrelated assets, LPs are exposed to genuine impermanent loss (unlike pegged StableSwap pools); the dynamic-peg profit/loss accounting and fees are designed so accumulated trading fees and concentration gains can offset that divergence loss.

KEY FACTS:

TOPIC: Balancer Weighted Pools: Invariant, Spot Price, Out-Given-In, Constant-Product Special Case, Weighted Impermanent Loss, and Use Cases

SUMMARY:
Balancer (Martinelli & Mushegian, whitepaper v2019-09-19) generalizes the constant-product AMM to an N-token Geometric Mean Market Maker (G3M). Its bedrock is the value function V = \prod_t B_t^{W_t}, where B_t is the on-chain balance of token t and W_t is its normalized weight with \sum_t W_t = 1 (whitepaper Eq. 1). Constraining V to a constant defines an invariant-value surface. A central proven property is that this invariant makes each token's share of total pool value constant and equal to its weight: the value of token n in units of any numeraire t is V_n^t = B_t \cdot W_n/W_t, the total is V^t = B_t/W_t, hence the value share S_n = V_n^t/V^t = W_n (Eqs. 8-10). Thus a Balancer pool behaves like a self-rebalancing index fund whose target portfolio weights are enforced by arbitrageurs rather than by a paid manager.

The spot price between an input token i (sold into the pool) and output token o (bought from the pool) is the ratio of weight-normalized balances, SP_i^o = (B_i/W_i)/(B_o/W_o) (Eq. 2); it is the limiting effective price EP_i^o = A_i/A_o as trade size goes to zero, and is recovered as the negative derivative -\partial B_i/\partial B_o of the invariant surface (Eqs. 3-7). The closed-form trade output, out-given-in, follows from requiring V to be unchanged by a swap (V actually rises only by the fee): A_o = B_o\,(1 - (B_i/(B_i+A_i))^{W_i/W_o}) (Eq. 15), and the dual in-given-out is A_i = B_i((B_o/(B_o-A_o))^{W_o/W_i}-1) (Eq. 20). Only the two traded tokens' balances and weights appear, so any pair within an N-token pool prices and trades independently. A swap fee f is applied by replacing the input with the post-fee amount A_i(1-f) inside the out-given-in expression, and it scales the quoted spot price by 1/(1-f).

The Uniswap-V2 constant-product rule is the equal-weight two-token special case. Setting W_i = W_o = 1/2 makes the invariant B_i^{1/2}B_o^{1/2} = const, i.e. B_i B_o = k, and the exponent W_i/W_o = 1 collapses out-given-in to A_o = B_o\,A_i/(B_i + A_i) (the fee-augmented form A_o = B_o\,(1-f)A_i/(B_i+(1-f)A_i) reproduces the familiar 997/1000 Uniswap-V2 quote with f=0.003). Spot price reduces to B_i/B_o. So Uniswap V2 is exactly the 50/50 Balancer pool.

For a G3M, the value-of-pool versus value-of-hold ratio after a price move from p_i to p_i' is value_pool/value_hold = \prod_i (p_i'/p_i)^{w_i} / (\sum_i w_i (p_i'/p_i)) — a weighted geometric mean over a weighted arithmetic mean of the per-asset gross returns, with the geometric-versus-arithmetic mean gap (AM-GM inequality) guaranteeing the ratio is at most 1, i.e. impermanent loss is always non-positive (this general framework is rigorously derived for AMMs including Balancer in Fukasawa, Maire & Wunsch 2022, arXiv:2203.11352, and Tassy & White's note for the 50/50 case). Concentrating weight on the asset that moves sharply reduces IL: for a single asset moving by factor r against a stable numeraire, IL = r^{w}/(w\,r + (1-w)) - 1. For a 2x move, a 50/50 pool loses 5.72% while an 80/20 pool (with the moving asset at w=0.8) loses only 3.27%; for a 5x move, 50/50 loses 25.46%, 80/20 loses 13.72%, and 95/5 loses just 3.89% — matching Martinelli's published 25.4% vs 3.88% figures. These properties drive Balancer's three flagship use cases: multi-asset index/portfolio pools that auto-rebalance to fixed target weights; 80/20 pools (e.g. 80% project token / 20% ETH) that let a project retain heavy exposure to its own token while still earning swap fees with much-reduced IL, popular for protocol-owned liquidity and governance-token staking; and Liquidity Bootstrapping Pools (LBPs), which use time-varying weights (e.g. starting 90/10 or 96/4 project/collateral and linearly flipping toward the collateral) to run a continuous Dutch-auction-style fair launch that suppresses front-running bots and whale sniping during price discovery.

KEY FACTS:
 - Balancer's value function is V = \prod_t B_t^{W_t} with \sum_t W_t = 1 (whitepaper Eq. 1); it is a Geometric Mean Market Maker generalizing the 2-asset constant product to N tokens with arbitrary weights.
 - Spot price is SP_i^o = (B_i/W_i)/(B_o/W_o) (Eq. 2), depending only on the two tokens' balances and weights; it equals the zero-size limit of the effective price A_i/A_o.
 - The invariant forces each token's value share to equal its weight, S_n = W_n (Eqs. 8-10), making the pool a self-rebalancing index fund maintained by arbitrageurs, not paid managers.
 - Out-given-in: A_o = B_o(1 - (B_i/(B_i+A_i))^{W_i/W_o}) (Eq. 15); in-given-out: A_i = B_i((B_o/(B_o-A_o))^{W_o/W_i} - 1) (Eq. 20).
 - A swap fee f is applied by substituting (1-f)A_i for A_i in out-given-in, and multiplies the quoted spot price by 1/(1-f).
 - Constant product is the equal-weight 50/50 two-token case: W_i=W_o=1/2 gives B_i B_o = k, and the out-given-in collapses to A_o = B_o(1-f)A_i/(B_i+(1-f)A_i), reproducing Uniswap V2's 997/1000 quote at f=0.003.
 - Weighted IL ratio is value_pool/value_hold = \prod_i (p_i'/p_i)^{w_i} / (\sum_i w_i (p_i'/p_i)) — weighted geometric over weighted arithmetic mean of gross returns; AM-GM guarantees the ratio is <=1 (IL always non-positive).
 - For a single asset moving against a stable numeraire, IL(w,r) = r^w/(w r + 1 - w) - 1; verified numerically.
 - For a 2x price move, a 50/50 pool's IL is -5.72% versus only -3.27% for an 80/20 pool (moving asset weighted 80%) — roughly 43% less IL.
 - For a 5x move, computed IL is -25.46% (50/50), -13.72% (80/20), and -3.89% (95/5), matching Martinelli's published 25.4% and 3.88% figures.
 - 80/20 pools let a project hold heavy exposure to its own token while earning swap fees with far lower IL; widely used for protocol-owned liquidity and governance-token staking.
 - Liquidity Bootstrapping Pools (LBPs) use time-varying weights (e.g. starting ~96/4 project/collateral and linearly flipping toward the collateral) to run a continuous Dutch-auction-style fair token launch that resists bots and whale sniping during price discovery.

TOPIC: Cross-cutting slippage / price impact comparison across Constant-Product (Uniswap V2/V3), StableSwap (Curve), and Weighted (Balancer) AMMs

SUMMARY:
Slippage (price impact) in a constant-function market maker (CFMM) is fully determined by the curvature of its trading invariant evaluated at the current reserve point. For a constant-product market (Uniswap V2), Angeris et al. give the trade constraint (R_alpha - Delta_alpha)(R_beta + gamma*Delta_beta) = k with fee 1-gamma, and the fee-free marginal (spot) price m_u = R_beta/R_alpha obtained by differentiating the invariant. If a trade consumes a fraction f = Delta_in/R_in of the input reserve, the fee-free output is Delta_out = R_out * f/(1+f), the realized average price is the spot price scaled by 1/(1+f), and the post-trade marginal price moves by the factor 1/(1+f)^2. Expanding, the marginal price impact is -2f + 3f^2 - ... : it grows ROUGHLY LINEARLY in trade size for small f and becomes CONVEX (super-linear) for large f, diverging as f -> infinity (you can never drain the pool). Slippage scales inversely with reserves/depth, so a 2x deeper pool halves the impact of a fixed-notional trade. This is the universal baseline against which the other designs deepen or thin liquidity.

Curve's StableSwap (Egorov) blends a constant-sum invariant (sum x_i = D, zero slippage) with a constant-product invariant (prod x_i = (D/n)^n) using a dynamic leverage factor chi = A*n^n*prod(x_i)/D^n, yielding the boxed invariant A*n^n*sum(x_i) + D = A*D*n^n + D^(n+1)/(n^n*prod x_i). NEAR THE PEG the pool is balanced, chi is at its maximum (~A) and DOMINATES, so the invariant is effectively the flat constant-sum line: marginal price stays ~1 and slippage is near zero — a trade of fraction f experiences O(f/A) impact rather than O(f), i.e. the amplification A flattens the curve and effectively multiplies the depth by roughly A. As the pool IMBALANCES, prod(x_i) collapses, chi -> 0, and the invariant degenerates to the constant-product branch, so slippage BLOWS UP toward Uniswap-like (and worse, because reserves on the depleted side are small) behavior. Thus A trades range (flatness/depth near peg) for tail risk (cliff when a peg breaks and the pool runs to one side).

The three knobs — Uniswap V3's concentrated liquidity, Curve's amplification A, and Balancer's weights — are all LOCAL liquidity-shaping parameters that change the marginal-slippage curve without changing the CFMM family qualitatively. V3 lets an LP confine liquidity L to a price band [p_a, p_b]; inside the band the position behaves like a V2 pool with VIRTUAL reserves x_v = L/sqrt(p), y_v = sqrt(p)*L that are much larger than the real deposited reserves, so the effective depth (and the local 1/(1+f) slippage) is amplified by concentration — a band that is k times narrower gives ~k times the depth and ~1/k the slippage, at the cost of slippage discontinuously worsening once price exits the band. Curve's A multiplies depth near the peg as above. Balancer's spot price SP = (B_i/W_i)/(B_o/W_o) and out-given-in A_out = B_o(1-(B_i/(B_i+A_in))^(W_i/W_o)) show that the weight ratio W_i/W_o rescales how a given balance change moves price: raising the weight of the asset being traded flattens its local price response (deeper for that asset), so an 80/20 pool offers far lower slippage on the 80%-weight token than a 50/50 pool with the same TVL, while concentrating risk.

QUALITATIVE SLIPPAGE ORDERING. For a SMALL trade on a PEGGED stablecoin pair, StableSwap (Curve) << Weighted (if tuned) ~ Constant-product, because A delivers near-zero slippage at the peg; Uniswap V3 with a tight range around 1.0 can match or beat Curve locally. For a SMALL trade on a VOLATILE pair, all reduce to their local marginal price and differences are minor (V3 concentrated > V2 full-range in depth; StableSwap is inappropriate and would be badly imbalanced). For a LARGE trade on a PEGGED pair, StableSwap is best only while it stays near balance; a large enough trade pushes it onto the constant-product branch where its slippage can EXCEED Uniswap's (small depleted-side reserves), and V3 incurs a slippage cliff at range edges. For a LARGE trade on a VOLATILE pair, full-range constant-product is the most robust (no cliffs, smooth convex impact), V3 is cheapest in-range but suffers when price leaves the band, and weighted pools sit in between depending on weights. The unifying principle: slippage = (trade size / local depth) modulated by curvature; depth and liquidity are increased locally by A, by concentration L within a band, and by weight on the traded asset, and slippage is the inverse of that local depth.

KEY FACTS:
 - Constant-product slippage as a fraction f of input reserves: output = y*f/(1+f), average price degrades by 1/(1+f), marginal price moves by 1/(1+f)^2, expanding to -2f + 3f^2 - ... (linear for small trades, convex for large).
 - Angeris et al. give the V2 invariant (R_alpha - Delta_alpha)(R_beta + gamma*Delta_beta) = k and the marginal price m_u = R_beta/R_alpha, with no-arbitrage bound gamma*m_p <= m_u <= gamma^{-1}*m_p (default gamma = 0.997).
 - A constant-product pool can never be fully drained: as trade size f -> infinity the price impact diverges, the structural reason slippage is convex.
 - Curve StableSwap invariant: A*n^n*sum(x_i) + D = A*D*n^n + D^(n+1)/(n^n*prod(x_i)), blending constant-sum (sum x_i = D) and constant-product (prod x_i = (D/n)^n).
 - The leverage factor chi = A*n^n*prod(x_i)/D^n equals A at perfect balance (flat, near-zero slippage) and decays toward 0 as the pool imbalances, at which point slippage degenerates to constant-product behavior — this is why slippage 'blows up' off-peg.
 - Amplification A effectively multiplies local depth near the peg by roughly A, widening the low-slippage band; larger A means flatter curve but a sharper cliff when a stablecoin de-pegs.
 - Balancer value function V = prod(B_i^W_i) with sum W_i = 1; spot price SP = (B_i/W_i)/(B_o/W_o); constant-product is the special 50/50 two-asset case.
 - Balancer weight ratio W_i/W_o rescales local price response: an 80/20 pool gives much lower slippage on the high-weight token than a 50/50 pool at the same TVL, concentrating risk on that asset.
 - Uniswap V3 confines liquidity L to a band [p_a, p_b]; in-range it trades on virtual reserves x_v = L/sqrt(p), y_v = L*sqrt(p) that exceed real reserves, amplifying depth ~ inversely to band width, but introduces a slippage discontinuity at range edges.
 - Qualitative ordering (small trade, pegged pair): Curve StableSwap << tuned Balancer ~ Constant-product; a tight-range V3 around 1.0 can match Curve locally.
 - Qualitative ordering (large trade, pegged pair): Curve is best only while near balance; a sufficiently large trade pushes it onto the constant-product branch where slippage can EXCEED Uniswap's because the depleted-side reserve is small.
 - Unifying principle: slippage ~ trade_size / (2 * local_depth); local depth is scaled by reserves R (V2), A*R near the peg (Curve), concentrated L in-range (V3), and (W_i/W_o)*R (Balancer).

TOPIC: LP Returns, Impermanent Loss, and Loss-Versus-Rebalancing (LVR) across Constant-Product, Weighted, and Stable AMMs

SUMMARY:
Impermanent loss (IL), also called divergence loss or more precisely loss-versus-holding, is the relative shortfall of a liquidity-provider (LP) position against simply HODLing the initial basket of assets. For a 50/50 constant-product (Uniswap V2) pool it has the closed form IL(r) = 2*sqrt(r)/(1+r) - 1, where r is the terminal price ratio. Because it follows from the AM-GM inequality (2*sqrt(r) <= 1+r), IL is always non-positive, is symmetric in r and 1/r, and is strictly path-independent: it depends only on entry and exit prices. Direct evaluation gives -0.62% at r=1.25, -2.02% at r=1.5, -5.72% at r=2, and -20.0% at r=4, growing superlinearly with the size of the price move. A LP's net profit relative to HODL is the sum of accumulated trading fees and the (negative) divergence loss, so the LP outperforms holding only when the cumulative fee yield F/V_hold meets or exceeds the IL fraction 1 - 2*sqrt(r)/(1+r); this is the standard break-even condition.\n\nLoss-versus-rebalancing (LVR), introduced by Milionis, Moallemi, Roughgarden and Zhang (arXiv:2208.06046, 2022), reframes LP cost as the rate at which arbitrageurs pick off stale AMM quotes relative to a continuously-rebalancing reference portfolio that always holds the same mix at the true market price. Modeling the external price as a driftless geometric Brownian motion with volatility sigma, they derive a Black-Scholes-style result: the instantaneous LVR rate is ell(P) = (1/2) sigma^2 P^2 |x'(P)| = -(1/2) sigma^2 P^2 V''(P), where x(P) is the pool's optimal risky-asset reserve and V(P) is the pool value function. LVR thus scales quadratically with volatility and price and linearly with marginal liquidity (the convexity/second derivative of the value function). For the constant-product pool this collapses to the celebrated price-independent constant: normalized instantaneous LVR equals sigma^2/8, i.e. ell = (sigma^2/8) * V(P) with V(P)=2*sqrt(kP). Integrated over time, cumulative LVR is exactly the floating leg of a marginal-liquidity-weighted variance swap, making explicit that it is realized variance (quadratic variation), not net displacement, that drains LP value. The accounting identity is LP value = rebalancing-portfolio value - cumulative LVR + fees.\n\nThe two notions of loss diverge sharply in their dynamics, which is the central conceptual point. IL is a terminal, path-independent quantity that fully reverts to zero if the price round-trips back to entry. LVR is a continuous, monotone non-decreasing running cost that accrues trade-by-trade to arbitrageurs (and is realized as MEV); it is strictly positive whenever the price path has nonzero variance, including on a round trip where IL nets to zero. In the authors' words, IL is better described as loss-versus-holding and is not a true running cost, whereas LVR is the genuine adverse-selection cost of market making. LVR therefore upper-bounds, and is a cleaner driver of, the realized underperformance of an appropriately delta-hedged LP.\n\nAcross AMM designs the magnitude of both IL and LVR is governed by the pool's curvature and weighting. Weighted geometric-mean pools (Balancer) have IL = (product of r_i^{w_i})/(sum of w_i r_i) - 1 and normalized LVR = (sigma^2/2) w(1-w); for w=1/2 this recovers sigma^2/8, while an 80/20 pool has w(1-w)=0.16 versus 0.25, roughly a 36% reduction in LVR and IL exposure per dollar (at the cost of higher slippage on the thin side). Stablecoin and pegged pools using Curve's StableSwap invariant operate on a nearly flat region of the bonding curve near the peg, where marginal liquidity is enormous but price barely moves, so IL and LVR are minimal as long as the peg holds. Uniswap V3 concentrated liquidity is the opposite extreme: confining capital to a narrow range multiplies effective marginal liquidity within the band, amplifying both fee income and LVR/IL by the same concentration factor, and empirical studies find roughly half of V3 LP positions underperform HODL after IL. Thus the IL/LVR ordering runs from near-zero (Curve near peg, Balancer high-weight asset) through Balancer 80/20, up to Uniswap V2 50/50, and is maximal for tightly-ranged Uniswap V3.

KEY FACTS:
 - Impermanent loss IL(r) = 2*sqrt(r)/(1+r) - 1 for a 50/50 constant-product pool is always <= 0, is symmetric in r and 1/r, and is path-independent (a function of terminal price ratio only).
 - IL table (verified by direct computation): r=1.25 -> -0.62%, r=1.5 -> -2.02%, r=2 -> -5.72%, r=4 -> -20.0%; a price halving (r=0.5) gives the same -5.72% as r=2.
 - Total LP profit-versus-HODL = accumulated fees F + IL*V_hold; the LP beats holding only when fee yield F/V_hold >= 1 - 2*sqrt(r)/(1+r) (e.g. 5.72% fee yield needed to offset a 2x move).
 - LVR (Milionis, Moallemi, Roughgarden, Zhang, arXiv:2208.06046, 2022) is the rate at which arbitrageurs extract value from LPs relative to a continuously-rebalancing reference strategy; it is the LP's adverse-selection cost from stale AMM prices.
 - General instantaneous LVR rate under GBM with volatility sigma: ell(P) = (1/2) sigma^2 P^2 |x'(P)| = -(1/2) sigma^2 P^2 V''(P); it scales quadratically in sigma and price and linearly in marginal liquidity.
 - Constant-product (Uniswap V2) headline result: normalized instantaneous LVR ell/V(P) = sigma^2/8, independent of price; equivalently ell = (sigma^2/8) * V(P) with V(P) = 2*sqrt(kP).
 - Cumulative LVR = integral of the rate = the floating leg of a marginal-liquidity-weighted variance swap, so LVR is driven by realized variance (quadratic variation), not net price displacement.
 - Accounting identity: LP value = rebalancing-portfolio value - cumulative LVR + fees; LVR is a monotone non-decreasing running cost.
 - Cross-AMM ordering of IL/LVR exposure: stablecoin/pegged pairs (Curve near peg) ~ negligible; Balancer 80/20 < 50/50; Uniswap V3 concentrated liquidity amplifies both fees and IL/LVR versus V2; the amplification grows as the range narrows.
 - Weighted geometric-mean pools (Balancer) have normalized LVR = (sigma^2/2) w(1-w); w=0.5 recovers sigma^2/8, and an 80/20 pool (w(1-w)=0.16) carries ~36% less LVR per dollar than 50/50 (0.25).
 - Weighted-pool IL = (product of r_i^{w_i}) / (sum of w_i r_i) - 1; the higher a token's weight, the smaller the IL it suffers when that token's price moves.
 - Key distinction: IL is path-independent and terminal (reverts to zero if price returns to entry), whereas LVR is a continuous running cost that accrues trade-by-trade to arbitrageurs/MEV and is strictly positive whenever the price has nonzero variance, even on a round trip.

TOPIC: Recent (2022-2025) AMM developments: Uniswap v4, LVR/MEV-mitigation AMMs (FM-AMM, am-AMM, CoW AMM), Curve V2 Cryptoswap, real-world TVL/volume scale, and dynamic fees / JIT liquidity

SUMMARY:
Uniswap v4 (whitepaper "Uniswap v4 Core", Adams et al., August 2024) does not change the underlying concentrated-liquidity invariant of v3 but re-architects the protocol around four extensibility primitives. (1) Hooks are externally deployed contracts that the pool calls out to at specified points in a pool's lifecycle; v4 defines exactly ten callbacks: beforeInitialize/afterInitialize, beforeAddLiquidity/afterAddLiquidity, beforeRemoveLiquidity/afterRemoveLiquidity, beforeSwap/afterSwap, and beforeDonate/afterDonate. The hook contract's ADDRESS encodes a bitmap of which callbacks are enabled, making dispatch gas-efficient. Hooks can natively implement dynamic/volatility-based fees, TWAMM, on-chain limit orders, custom oracles, MEV-internalization mechanisms, and even constant-product (v2-style) curves via Custom Accounting. (2) A singleton design holds all pools in one contract, cutting pool-creation gas by ~99% and cheapening multi-hop swaps. (3) Flash accounting tracks only net balances (deltas) during a transaction and enforces solvency (no tokens owed) at the end via take()/settle(); it is implemented with EIP-1153 transient storage. (4) ERC-6909 multi-token claims let users/hooks keep balances inside the singleton, avoiding ERC-20 transfers; native ETH support is restored. Collectively v4 turns the AMM into a programmable platform rather than a fixed curve.

A major 2022-2025 research theme is Loss-Versus-Rebalancing (LVR), introduced by Milionis, Moallemi, Roughgarden and Zhang (2022). LVR isolates the adverse-selection cost a CFMM LP pays to better-informed arbitrageurs who pick off stale pool prices, separate from inventory/IL risk. Under geometric Brownian motion the instantaneous LVR rate is one-half the squared volatility times the price-sensitivity of the optimal (rebalanced) holdings; it scales quadratically in volatility and with the curvature of the pool's demand curve, and for a constant-product pool grows roughly linearly in time. Several mechanism designs target LVR/MEV: the function-maximizing AMM (FM-AMM, Canidio & Fritsch, arXiv:2307.02074, 2023) batches all trades in a block and executes them at a single uniform clearing price so that competing arbitrageurs drive the price to the external market price, eliminating both LVR-style arbitrage rents and sandwich attacks; CoW Protocol's CoW AMM (live on Balancer, 2024) is the production realization of this idea — solvers compete in batch auctions and the solver offering the most surplus wins the right to rebalance the pool, so arbitrage value flows back to LPs ("MEV-capturing AMM"). The am-AMM (Adams, Moallemi, Reynolds, Robinson, arXiv:2403.03367, 2024) instead auctions, via a censorship-resistant on-chain English/rental auction, the temporary right to be the pool manager, who sets the swap fee and collects all swap fees; the manager internalizes small-price-movement arbitrage and tunes fees to retail order-flow elasticity, with proceeds accruing to LPs. Under their assumptions the am-AMM sustains higher equilibrium liquidity than any fixed-fee AMM, and it is explicitly cited as a candidate v4 hook.

Curve V2 / Cryptoswap (2021, extending the 2019-2020 StableSwap design) generalizes Curve to volatile, non-pegged pairs. Its invariant interpolates between the constant-sum (StableSwap-like, near-flat, low-slippage) regime when the pool is balanced and the constant-product (Uniswap-like, higher-slippage) regime when imbalanced, governed by an amplification parameter A and a gamma parameter that controls the width of the concentrated region. Crucially, liquidity is concentrated around an internal EMA price oracle rather than a fixed peg, and an automatic "repegging" procedure shifts the price scale toward the observed market price only when the accumulated profit justifies the move, giving Uniswap-v3-like capital efficiency without manual range management. This made Curve V2 the venue for volatile pairs (e.g., tricrypto: ETH/BTC/USD), complementing v1's stablecoin focus.

On real-world scale (approximate, ~2025-2026 snapshots from DefiLlama; figures fluctuate): Uniswap is the dominant DEX by TVL and volume — aggregate Uniswap TVL is in the multi-billion range (DefiLlama showed roughly US$2.8B on the consolidated Uniswap page and third-party trackers cite ~US$4.5B across all chains/versions mid-2025), with 30-day DEX volume on the order of tens of billions of USD; Uniswap v4 reportedly reached US$1B TVL within ~177 days of its early-2025 launch. Curve Finance TVL is roughly US$1.4B (heavily on Ethereum). Balancer is much smaller, with TVL on the order of US$100-130M. These figures are time-sensitive and should be cited as approximate snapshots, not fixed values. Finally, dynamic fees and just-in-time (JIT) liquidity are tightly linked: JIT is a concentrated-liquidity MEV strategy where an LP, seeing a large pending swap in the mempool, mints a tight-range position immediately before it and burns it immediately after to capture nearly all the swap's fees while crowding out passive LPs (studies report large dilution of incumbent LP fee share on the targeted swaps, though JIT is rare and capital-intensive overall). Dynamic-fee hooks (now native in v4) are one defense, raising fees during volatile/toxic flow to compensate passive LPs for LVR and to reduce the profitability of JIT and arbitrage extraction.

[Slippage / price-impact profile]
  CP: Convex, ~linear small trades; unbounded as size grows; pool never fully drained
  SS: Near-zero near peg (depth ~A*R); cliffs to product behavior off-peg
  WP: Scaled by W_i/W_o; high-weight token sees lower slippage at same TVL

[Capital efficiency]
  CP: V2 full-range, low; V3 concentrated band gives up to ~4000x amplification
  SS: High near peg; A multiplies local depth ~A-fold within the flat band
  WP: Like V2 (full-range); 80/20 skews depth toward the high-weight asset

[Impermanent loss profile]
  CP: IL=2*sqrt(r)/(1+r)-1; -5.72% at 2x, -20% at 4x; V3 amplifies in-range
  SS: Tiny for pegged assets; only material if the peg actually breaks
  WP: IL=r^w/(w*r+1-w)-1; 80/20 ~43% less IL than 50/50 at 2x

[Fee model]
  CP: V2 0.3% on input, compounds into k; V3 tiers 0.01/0.05/0.3/1%, non-compounding
  SS: Low static (~0.04-0.06%) StableSwap; V2 dynamic fee scaling with imbalance
  WP: Single swap fee f applied as (1-f)*A_i in out-given-in formula

[Ideal use case]
  CP: V2 any long-tail pair; V3 active LPs wanting concentrated, capital-efficient ranges
  SS: Mutually pegged/correlated assets (stablecoins, LSTs); V2 for volatile pairs
  WP: Multi-asset index pools, 80/20 protocol-owned liquidity, LBP token launches

[Notable risk / limitation]
  CP: Divergence loss and LVR; V3 adds out-of-range inactivity and fee-management burden
  SS: Depeg risk: LPs absorb the bad asset; off-peg slippage can exceed product pools
  WP: Still suffers IL/LVR; LBP and high-weight pools concentrate single-asset risk

################################################################################
KEY TAKEAWAYS:
 - All three families are constant-function market makers differing only in their trading function: Uniswap uses x*y=k, Balancer the weighted geometric mean prod(B_i^W_i), and Curve the StableSwap blend of constant-sum and constant-product tuned by amplification A.
 - Slippage is governed by one unifying rule, slippage ~ trade_size/(2*local_depth): local depth equals reserves R for V2, ~A*R near the peg for Curve, concentrated L in-range for V3, and (W_i/W_o)*R for Balancer.
 - Curve dominates for pegged assets near balance (near-zero slippage and tiny IL), but once a stablecoin depegs the leverage factor chi collapses, the curve reverts to constant-product, and off-peg slippage can exceed Uniswap's.
 - Uniswap V3 concentrated liquidity buys huge capital efficiency (up to ~4000x) at the cost of active range management, out-of-range inactivity (position becomes single-asset and stops earning), and amplified in-range impermanent loss.
 - Impermanent loss is a function of terminal price ratio only: 50/50 pools give IL=2*sqrt(r)/(1+r)-1 (-5.72% at 2x), while Balancer's weighting lets an 80/20 pool cut IL roughly 43% by concentrating exposure on one asset.
 - LVR (Milionis et al.) reframes LP cost as adverse selection from stale AMM prices versus a continuously rebalancing strategy, with instantaneous rate (1/2)*sigma^2*P^2*|x'(P)|; it is design-agnostic and motivates fee-aware, batch, and auction-managed AMM responses.
 - Fee mechanics diverge structurally: V2 compounds 0.3% fees into k (gas-cheap, ERC-20 LP shares), V3 accrues non-compounding per-position fees (NFTs, multiple tiers), Curve uses low or dynamic fees, and Balancer applies a single configurable swap fee.
 - Use-case fit follows the math: constant-product for volatile/long-tail pairs, StableSwap for correlated/pegged baskets, and weighted pools for multi-asset index exposure, 80/20 protocol-owned liquidity, and Dutch-auction LBP token launches.
